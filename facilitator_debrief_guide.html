Before You Begin
Prepare the environment · Read the diagnostic · Pray
15 min before the meeting
📖

Read the diagnostic completely

Before the person arrives, read all their responses — not just the scores. Underline anything surprising, concerning, or worth affirming. The open-text questions (A1, A3, A5, B2, B5, H1–H6) are your richest material.

🙏

Pray specifically for this person

Ask God for discernment, wisdom, and compassion. Ask Him to show you what He is doing in this person's life — and what He wants you to say and not say. You are a co-laborer, not the Holy Spirit.

🪑

Set the environment

Choose a private setting. Silence your phone. Have water or coffee. Sit beside or across — not behind a desk. This is a pastoral conversation, not an evaluation panel. Warmth before everything.

⚠️

Know what you are NOT doing

You are not grading them. You are not approving or rejecting them. You are not the judge of their soul. You are a witness to what God is doing — and a guide toward the next step. Stay humble and stay curious.

🎯

Identify 2–3 priority areas

Based on what you read, decide before they arrive which sections need the most time. You cannot go deep on everything in 90 minutes. Pick the areas of greatest concern or greatest joy and prioritize those.

💬

The listening posture

You should be speaking less than 30% of the time. Your job is to ask, listen, reflect, and occasionally affirm or gently challenge. The person across from you needs to be heard more than they need to be taught — at least in this meeting.

A
The Gospel Event
Rom 10:9–10 · Eph 2:8–9 · John 3:16
~12 min
Your goal: Determine whether this person understands the gospel accurately and has made a genuine personal response to it — not just a cultural, familial, or emotional one.
Opening Start with gratitude and set the tone ▼
"[Name], thank you for taking the time to fill this out honestly. I read everything you wrote, and I want you to know — this conversation is completely safe. There are no right answers I'm looking for. I just want to understand where you are and how I can best walk alongside you. Can we start with a word of prayer?"
Pray briefly. Keep it conversational and warm — not a performance. Ask God to guide the conversation and to meet this person right where they are.
A1 · Gospel Knowledge Have them explain the gospel to you ▼
"In your written answer about the gospel, you said [quote a phrase from their A1 response]. Can you tell me more about that? If I had never heard of Jesus, how would you explain the good news to me right now, in your own words?"
Listen for the four essential gospel elements:
  • God — who He is, His holiness and love
  • Humanity — sin, separation, our inability to save ourselves
  • Jesus — His identity, His substitutionary death, His resurrection
  • Response — repentance and faith, not just intellectual agreement
✅ Healthy sign

They explain all four elements naturally, in their own language, with personal ownership — not as a recited formula. They connect the gospel to their own story.

🚩 Flag for follow-up

They focus only on God's love or Jesus' death without mentioning sin, repentance, or faith. Or they describe salvation as being a good person, following the church's teachings, or getting baptized. If this happens, gently ask: "What do you think a person needs to do to be saved?" — and listen very carefully to the answer.

Your notes on their gospel understanding:
A2–A3 · The Decision Explore the moment of conversion ▼
"You wrote about [summarize their A2/A3 response]. Tell me about that moment. What do you remember feeling and thinking? What did you understand you were saying yes to?"
You are not looking for a dramatic conversion story. Many genuine believers came to faith gradually. What you are listening for is:
  • A sense of genuine personal need — they understood they were a sinner
  • A conscious turning toward Christ — not just a prayer repeated after someone
  • Some awareness of what they were receiving — forgiveness, a new relationship with God
  • Evidence of lasting life change since that moment
If they said the decision was "gradual" — that's fine. Ask: "Can you point to a season when you knew something had genuinely changed?"
🚩 Flag for follow-up

They describe accepting Jesus because of peer pressure, family expectation, or an emotional moment at a camp — with no evidence of understanding or lasting change. Or they have no memory of any decision at all. This does not mean they are unsaved — it means you need to go deeper. Do not rush past this.

Your notes on their conversion account:
A5 · The Basis Question — Most Revealing "Why would God let you into heaven?" ▼
"Here's a question I want to explore with you from what you wrote. If you stood before God today and He asked you 'Why should I let you into heaven?' — what would your honest answer be?"
This is the single most diagnostic question in the entire debrief. Their answer instantly reveals whether they are trusting in Christ or in their own righteousness.
✅ Grace-based answer — proceed with confidence

"Because of what Jesus did for me." / "Because Christ died for my sins and I have trusted Him." / "Not because of anything I've done — only because of God's grace through Jesus."

🚩 Works-based or mixed answer — requires deeper exploration

"Because I've tried to be a good person." / "Because I go to church and serve." / "Because I was baptized." / "Because I love God and try to follow His commands." — Any answer that includes human effort as a basis (not a fruit) of acceptance signals that the gospel has not yet fully taken root. This may require you to present the gospel clearly before closing the meeting.

B
Assurance of Salvation
1 John 5:11–13 · Rom 8:15–16 · John 10:28–29
~10 min
Your goal: Distinguish between grace-based assurance, performance-based uncertainty, and dangerous presumption — and address each appropriately.
B1–B3 · Exploring Assurance Where does their confidence come from? ▼
"You rated your confidence at [their B1 score] out of 5. Tell me more about that. What does your assurance — or your uncertainty — actually rest on day to day?"
  • If confident: "What would you say to someone who told you that confidence is arrogant?"
  • If uncertain: "When you feel unsure about your salvation, what usually triggered that feeling?"
  • If very uncertain: "Has there ever been a season when you felt sure? What changed?"
"When you sin or fail morally — what's your first instinct? What does your gut tell you to do?"
Their answer to this question (B4 in the diagnostic) is one of the most revealing in the entire assessment. A person who runs to God after sin (confession, 1 John 1:9) is very different from one who runs away from God (performance recovery, avoidance, shame spiral).
🚩 Performance-based uncertainty

They feel saved when they're doing well and unsaved when they sin. Their assurance fluctuates with their behavior. This is extremely common in Filipino church culture and is rooted in a works-based misunderstanding of grace. Key scripture to walk through together: Romans 8:1 and 1 John 1:9. Do not move on until this is addressed.

🚩 Presumption (the other danger)

They are extremely confident — but their life shows little evidence of transformation (low scores across C, D, E). This is a different problem: cheap grace. Ask gently: "What in your life has genuinely changed since you became a Christian?"

Notes on their assurance posture:
B5 · The Doubt Story Exploring their experience of doubt ▼
"You wrote about a moment of doubt [reference their B5 answer]. What did God use to help you through that? Or are you still in the middle of it?"
Doubt is not the enemy of faith — it is often the companion of genuine faith. The question is not whether they doubt, but what they do with the doubt. Do they bring it to God and His Word, or do they let it drive them away?
  • "What promises from the Bible have anchored you in moments of uncertainty?"
  • "Is there a person you talk to about your doubts — or do you mostly carry them alone?"

C
Fruit of the Spirit — Character
Gal 5:22–23 · Matt 7:16–20 · 2 Cor 5:17
~12 min
Your goal: Identify which fruits are visibly growing, which are absent or stunted, and whether the overall pattern suggests genuine Spirit-indwelling transformation.
C1–C9 · The Fruit Review Affirm growth, then explore the low scores ▼
"Looking at the fruit of the Spirit ratings — you scored yourself highest on [highest-rated fruit]. Tell me about that. Give me a recent, specific example of when that showed up in your life."
Always affirm first. Never start with the problem areas. The person needs to know you see what God is doing before you address what God is still working on.
"You also rated [lowest-rated fruit] as an area of struggle. What does that look like in your daily life? Can you give me a specific recent situation where you felt that weakness?"
  • "When did this become a pattern? Was there an event or season that contributed to it?"
  • "Have you ever talked to anyone about this struggle — or is this the first time?"
  • "What do you think God is using this struggle to do in you?"
  • "What would it look like to take one small step of growth in this area this month?"
The key interpretive question: Are the low scores accompanied by grief and a desire to grow? Or are they accompanied by indifference? Grief over sin is a mark of the Spirit (Rom 7:24). Indifference is not.
🚩 Multiple 1–2 scores across the fruit — especially Love, Faithfulness, Self-control

If the majority of fruits are rated 1–2 and the person shows no genuine concern about this, this is significant. Gently probe: "Are these areas you want to grow in, or do you feel fairly okay with where you are?" Their response will tell you a great deal.

Which fruits stood out — positively and negatively? Your observations:
C10–C11 · Growth and Struggle Let them narrate their own transformation ▼
"You wrote that [name the fruit they identified in C10] is most visibly growing. Walk me through the example you gave. How does that compare to who you were 2–3 years ago?"
The distance between "then" and "now" is where you see sanctification. Even modest growth over time is powerful evidence of the Spirit's work.
"You also identified [their C11 answer] as your greatest struggle. You said the root might be [their answer]. Do you feel that's accurate? Or as you sit here now, do you see something deeper?"
Sometimes the diagnostic surfaces a deeper root than the person initially identified. The debrief conversation — with the safety of pastoral presence — often reveals what the paper could not.
Notes on their growth trajectory and root issues:
D
The 1 John Tests
1 John 1:6–7 · 2:3–4 · 2:15 · 3:6 · 3:14 · 5:1
~10 min
Your goal: Surface any patterns of hidden sin, broken relationships, or worldliness that the written diagnostic may only have hinted at.
D1–D6 · Walking Through the Tests The tests of genuine faith ▼
"John's letter was written so that believers can know they have eternal life. He gives several repeating tests throughout it. I want to walk through a few of them with you briefly."
You do not need to cover all six tests in depth. Focus on the 1–2 tests where their score was lowest. The tests most worth exploring in a conversation are:
  • D1 (Walking in the light): "Is there anything in your life right now that you're keeping hidden — from God, from me, from the people who love you?" Pause. Wait. Let the silence work.
  • D4 (Practicing righteousness): "Is there a pattern of sin in your life right now that you haven't dealt with? Not a struggle — a pattern you've accepted?"
  • D5 (Loving believers): "Is there anyone in this church you are actively avoiding or have unresolved bitterness toward? You don't have to name them."
On D1: This question surfaces secrets. Create the safest possible environment before asking it. Speak softly. Make eye contact. Do not have your pen ready to write. What surfaces here may require pastoral care that extends far beyond this meeting.
🚩 Ongoing unconfessed sin or broken relationships

If the person discloses ongoing habitual sin (pornography, dishonesty at work, unforgiveness held for years, sexual sin), this is not a disqualifying revelation — it is a pastoral opportunity. Respond with grace, not shock. The fact that they are telling you is itself a sign of spiritual health. Address it pastorally before proceeding to the pipeline.

Notes (use extreme discretion with what you write here):
D7 · Broken Relationships Unresolved relational fractures ▼
"You mentioned [reference their D7 answer, or ask if they left it blank] a situation involving a broken relationship. Without naming the person — can you tell me a bit more about where things stand? How long have you been carrying this?"
  • "Have you taken any steps toward reconciliation, or has it felt impossible?"
  • "Is this someone in the church? Because Jesus is pretty clear in Matthew 5:23–24 that this matters to Him before worship."
  • "What would it take for you to take a first step — even a small one — toward this person this week?"
Note: Reconciliation is not always possible (Rom 12:18 — "as far as it depends on you"). But the willingness to forgive and the effort to reconcile are non-negotiable marks of a Spirit-filled disciple. Address this compassionately but clearly.

E
Spiritual Disciplines — The Habit Foundation
Josh 1:8 · Ps 1:2 · Luke 18:1 · Heb 10:25
~8 min
Your goal: Identify which spiritual disciplines are established, which are absent, and what one concrete habit to build this month.
E1–E8 · The Habits Review Don't scold — diagnose and build ▼
"Looking at your spiritual habits — what's working? Which of these disciplines feels most natural and consistent for you right now?"
Then identify the two or three lowest-scoring disciplines. Do not address all of them — that is overwhelming and counterproductive. Pick the one foundational habit that, if built, would most strengthen everything else. For most people at Level 1, that is daily Bible reading and journaling.
"You rated [lowest habit] at [score]. What's been getting in the way of that becoming more consistent for you?"
  • "Is it a time issue, a motivation issue, or a 'I don't know how' issue?"
  • "What does your morning/evening routine look like right now?"
  • "If we were to build just ONE new habit this month, what would make the biggest difference?"
The goal of this section is not a lecture on the disciplines. It is to help the person leave with ONE specific, achievable habit commitment — time, method, and accountability structure included. Write it in the notes below and include it in the follow-up plan.
"Before we move on — what is one habit you are willing to commit to this month? When, what, and how will you do it? And who will you tell so they can ask you about it?"
The one-habit commitment (be specific — day, time, method, accountability partner):
F
Evangelism & EOLO
Matt 28:19–20 · Acts 1:8 · 1 Pet 3:15
~8 min
Your goal: Confirm their EOLO commitment is active, identify any blocks to evangelism, and build confidence in sharing their testimony.
F3 · EOLO Check-in Who is their one person? ▼
"Tell me about your EOLO person. Who are you believing God for right now? What's their story — how do you know them?"
If they have not yet identified someone, do not shame them. Ask instead: "Who in your life comes to mind when you think about someone far from God — someone you genuinely care about?" Help them name one person before the meeting ends. This is non-negotiable.
  • "How long have you known them? What's your relationship like?"
  • "Have you been praying for them? What have you been praying?"
  • "Have you had any spiritual conversations with them yet?"
  • "What's the next step — what could you do this week to deepen the relationship?"
EOLO person details (confidential — first name or initials only):
F1–F2 · Testimony Practice Have them share their testimony right now ▼
"I'd love to hear your testimony — your personal story of coming to faith. Can you share it with me right now, in under 3 minutes, as if I were your EOLO person and you're telling me what happened to you?"
This is a live practice, not just a discussion. Let them do it. Don't interrupt. Afterward, give them two affirmations and one suggestion — never more than one area of growth in a single conversation.
  • "What I found most compelling in your testimony was [specific phrase]."
  • "One thing that could make it even more powerful: [specific suggestion — e.g., add more about life before Christ, include one concrete change you've noticed, make it more conversational]."
If they struggle significantly — they can't articulate their testimony clearly, or their story has significant gospel gaps — note this as a development priority. Their testimony is one of the most powerful evangelism tools they have.

G
Church & Community Integration
Acts 2:42–47 · Heb 10:24–25 · Rom 12:5
~6 min
Your goal: Assess whether this person is genuinely embedded in community or just attending. A lone-ranger Christian cannot be discipled or grow sustainably.
G1–G4 · Belonging, Serving, Accountability Do they belong, or just attend? ▼
"When you think about this church, do you feel like you belong here — or do you still feel like a visitor in some ways? What would help you feel more at home?"
  • "Who in this church would you call if you had a crisis at 11pm?"
  • "Are you currently in a Life Community group? What's that been like for you?"
  • "Is there someone in your life right now who asks you the hard questions — about your walk with God, your purity, your finances, your marriage? Who holds you accountable?"
The question "Who would you call at 11pm?" is one of the most accurate measures of genuine community. It bypasses polite church answers and goes straight to relational reality.
🚩 Isolation — no accountability, no deep friendships

A person who has been attending for more than 6 months and cannot name one genuine friendship is at significant spiritual risk. Before closing the meeting, make a specific connection: "I want to introduce you to [name] — I think you two would connect well. Can I arrange that?" Isolation is not a preference to be respected; it is a vulnerability to be addressed.

Notes on community depth and next steps:
H
Open Reflection — Their Unfiltered Voice
Ps 139:23–24 · Lam 3:40 · 2 Cor 13:5
~10 min
Your goal: Let the person's own words surface the most important themes. Often what they wrote in H1–H6 is the most important material in the entire diagnostic.
H1 · Spiritual Autobiography Reflect back what you heard in their story ▼
"You wrote your spiritual story in Section H. I read it carefully. One thing that struck me was [specific phrase or theme from their H1 response]. Can you tell me more about that season?"
The spiritual autobiography is not a section to rush through. It is where the person's whole journey is laid out. Look for:
  • Periods of distance from God — what caused them and what brought them back
  • Key people God used — disciplers, parents, friends
  • Formative pain — loss, failure, betrayal — and how they processed it spiritually
  • The current season — what is God doing right now in their story?
Key themes from their spiritual autobiography:
H2–H3 · Current Struggle & Resistance The most honest questions in the guide ▼
"You named [their H2 answer] as your greatest spiritual struggle right now. How long has this been going on? Is this something you've ever talked about with anyone before today?"
"And you said that God is calling you to change [their H3 answer], but you've been resisting it. That took honesty to write. What do you think the cost of that resistance has been — to you, to your relationships, to your walk with God?"
Do not offer solutions immediately. Let the weight of their own words sit with them for a moment. Then ask: "What would it look like to take one step of obedience in that area this week?"
H3 is particularly powerful because the person has already admitted to knowing what God wants and choosing not to do it. This is not a moment for condemnation — it is a moment for compassionate, clear pastoral truth-telling. Use it wisely.
Notes on their current struggle and area of resistance:
H4 · The Jesus Question "What would Jesus say to you right now?" ▼
"You wrote that if Jesus were sitting with you right now, He would say [their H4 response]. Does that still feel true as you sit here? What do you think He would add, knowing everything we've talked about today?"
This question often produces the most profound moment of the entire debrief. Whether their answer came from grace or from fear, the conversation now has context — you have spent 60+ minutes together — and their answer may shift. The quality of their answer to this question is one of your best final indicators of their spiritual health.
✅ Healthy answer — grace-rooted

"He would say He loves me, He's proud of me, and He's working in me — and He would challenge me to trust Him with [specific area]." This combination of acceptance and challenge reflects a mature grasp of the gospel.

🚩 Fear-rooted answer

"He would be disappointed in me." / "He would tell me I need to do better." — These answers reveal a performance-based relationship with God. They need the gospel preached to them — specifically the truth that Jesus' love is not conditioned on their performance. Do not let the meeting end without speaking this clearly.

Closing the Meeting & Determining the Outcome
Rom 15:14 · 2 Tim 2:2 · 1 Thess 2:11–12
~8 min
Closing Questions Before you determine the outcome ▼
"Before I share my observations, I want to ask you: as you've been talking through all of this today — what has stood out to you most? What do you feel God is saying to you through this conversation?"
Let them speak. This is one of the most important questions of the whole meeting. Often the person will summarize their own spiritual condition more accurately than any external assessment.
"Is there anything you want to say or ask that we haven't covered? Anything you've been holding back that you want me to know?"
Create one more opening before you transition to the outcome. Some people will share the most significant thing at this moment — when they sense the meeting is almost over and the window is closing.
What emerged in the closing questions:
Sharing Your Assessment — The Script How to communicate the outcome warmly and clearly ▼
Always begin your assessment with affirmation. Then name growth areas. Then give next steps. Never reverse this order.
"Can I share what I observed today — what I see God doing in you, and what I think the next step looks like?"
If the outcome is Clear Assurance:
"[Name], what I see today is someone with genuine, real faith in Jesus. The fruit of the Spirit is growing in you — maybe not perfectly, but it's there and it's real. Your understanding of the gospel is solid, and your desire to grow is evident. Here's what I'd like to suggest as your next step: [specific next step — BTLI 101, discipler assignment, LC group]. Are you ready to take that step?"
If the outcome requires more work:
"[Name], I want to be honest with you because I care about you. I see genuine things in your life — [specific affirmations]. And I also see some areas where I think we need to spend more time together before we move to the next step. Specifically: [one or two specific areas]. I don't say that to discourage you — I say it because I want your foundation to be solid before we build on it. Here's what I'd like to propose: [specific plan — additional meetings, specific scripture to work through, counseling referral]. Is that okay with you?"

Select the Official Outcome for This Person

✅
Clear Assurance
Strong gospel understanding, credible testimony, visible fruit, healthy habits. Proceed to Level 1 / BTLI 101 immediately.
🟡
Genuine but Uncertain
Salvation likely genuine but assurance is performance-based. 2–3 additional sessions on 1 John 5 and Romans 8 before Level 1.
🟠
Formation Gap
Faith credible but significant habit deficits, isolation, or character patterns need addressing. Begin Level 1 with intensive coaching support.
🔴
Unresolved
Gospel unclear, no credible conversion, or persistent unrepentant sin. Present the gospel clearly. Do not place in ministry. This is your highest priority relationship.

Next Steps — Clear Assurance

  • Assign a personal discipler within 7 days
  • Enroll in next BTLI 101 cohort — give them the start date today
  • Confirm they are in an active Life Community group
  • Follow up on their EOLO person — schedule a 30-day check-in
  • Celebrate with them — this moment deserves acknowledgment
  • File this completed guide in their pipeline record

Next Steps — Genuine but Uncertain

  • Schedule 2–3 additional one-on-one sessions focused on assurance
  • Assign: Read 1 John slowly, one chapter per week, journaling responses
  • Walk them through Romans 8:1, 8:15–16, and 8:38–39 together
  • Do not delay BTLI 101 unnecessarily — begin with assurance sessions alongside it
  • Pray together at every meeting for the Spirit to witness to their spirit (Rom 8:16)
  • Re-administer the B section of this diagnostic after 60 days

Next Steps — Formation Gap

  • Begin BTLI 101 but assign a discipler who meets weekly (not monthly)
  • Focus the first 30 days on one foundational habit only — don't overwhelm
  • Address the specific gap areas named in your notes above
  • If isolation is the primary gap — facilitate a specific LC group connection this week
  • If broken relationships are flagged — pastoral mediation or counseling referral
  • Check in personally every 2 weeks for the first quarter

Next Steps — Unresolved

  • Do not enroll in BTLI 101 yet — the foundation must come first
  • Present the gospel clearly and warmly at your next meeting — come prepared
  • Do not place in any ministry leadership role
  • Schedule 3 follow-up meetings — on the gospel, on repentance, on the cross
  • If ongoing unrepentant sin is present — address it directly and compassionately
  • Treat this person as your highest priority discipleship relationship right now
  • Pray daily for them by name — and tell them you are

Close Every Meeting in Prayer

Make clear what is still unclear. And help me to be the kind of shepherd who leads this person closer to You — not further from You. Amen."
